## SECTION 1: ORTHOGRAPHY & PHONOLOGY GUIDE

### Core Sound Substitutions
| English | Patois | Example |
|---------|--------|---------|
| th → | d or t | "the" → "di", "them" → "dem", "think" → "tink", "three" → "tree" |
| -ing → | -in | "running" → "runnin", "talking" → "talkin" |
| -er → | -a | "better" → "betta", "mother" → "madda", "water" → "wata" |
| wh → | w | "what" → "wah", "where" → "weh" |
| h (initial) | often dropped | "have" → "ave", "him" → "im", OR h added: "itch" → "heetch" |
| -tion → | -shun or -shan | "situation" → "sityashun" |

### Key Spelling Rules
- Double letters for emphasis: "likkle" (little), "nuff" (enough), "betta" (better)
- Vowel shifts: "girl" → "gyul/gyal", "car" → "kyar", "garden" → "gyarden"
- Contracted words: "about" → "bout", "going" → "goin", "looking" → "lookin"
- "a" as multipurpose preposition/aux verb: "a go" (going to), "a wah" (what is)
- Past tense markers: "did", "ben", "en" placed BEFORE the verb (not conjugating the verb itself)
- Future tense: "a go" before verb — "Mi a go eat" = "I am going to eat"
- Negation: "nuh", "cyaan", "nah", "no" — double negatives accepted and normal

## SECTION 2: GRAMMAR ESSENTIALS

### Pronouns
| English | Patois |
|---------|--------|
| I / me / my | mi / mi / fi mi |
| you / your | yuh / fi yuh |
| he / him / his | im / im / fi im |
| she / her | she / har / fi ar |
| we / us / our | wi / wi / fi wi |
| you all / your (pl) | unu / unu |
| they / them / their | dem / dem / fi dem |

*Rasta variant: "I and I" (InI) replaces I, me, we — emphasizing unity with Jah*

### Tense Markers
- **Present progressive:** "a" before verb → "Mi a run" = I am running
- **Future:** "a go" before verb → "Mi a go eat" = I am going to eat / "wi" = will
- **Past:** "did" before verb → "Mi did eat" = I ate / "ben"/"en" also used
- **Completed action:** "done" → "Mi done eat" = I have already eaten
- **Habitual/general:** "always" or no marker → "Mi run every day"

### Key Grammar Notes
- Copula (is/are) often dropped: "She pretty" = She is pretty
- "deh" = to be (location): "Mi deh yah" = I am here
- Plurality marked by "dem" after noun: "di book dem" = the books
- Question formed by tone, not word order: "Yuh a go?" = Are you going?
- "seh" = "that" (subordinator): "Mi know seh yuh deh yah" = I know that you are here
- Serial verbs common: "gi mi tek it" = give it to me to take

- **Waan** — want ("Mi waan eat" = I want to eat)
- **Wagwan/Wah gwaan** — What's going on? What's up? (primary greeting)
- **Walk good** — take care; used when parting ways. "Walk good, mi bredda"
- **Wanti wanti** — someone who desperately wants something they can't have
- **Weh** — where ("weh yuh deh?" = where are you?); also "what" in some contexts
- **Weh di party deh?** — Where's the party?
- **Weh yuh ah seh** — What are you saying? / How are you doing? (casual check-in)
- **Whola dem** — all of them ("di whola dem" = all of them)
- **Wicked** — excellent, amazing (inverted meaning like "bad")
- **Whole heap** — a lot, many ("whole heap a people deh deh")
- **Wutless** — worthless, no good ("yuh wutless!" = you're worthless!)

### Y
- **Y pree** — "Why pree?" / "You pree?" — dual meaning:
  - **As greeting:** What's up? What's going on? ("Y pree bredda?" = What's up, brother?)
  - **As dismissal:** Why are you in my business? Mind your own. ("Y pree? Stupid ting." = Why are you watching me?)
  - Root: "pree" (see P section). Derived from "you perceive" → "y pree" through creolization. Context determines which meaning fires.
- **Yah** — here ("mi deh yah" = I am here)
- **Yah mon** — yes man; no problem; sure thing (classic affirmation)
- **Yardie** — a Jamaican, used especially in diaspora communities
- **Yard** — home, one's place ("come a mi yard" = come to my house)
- **Yuh** — you ("yuh know?" = you know?)
- **Yute** — youth, young person ("di yute dem" = the young people)

### Z
- **Zeen** — "Seen" — I understand, got it, OK. Used to affirm understanding or agreement. ("Zeen, mi get yuh" = Got it, I understand you)
- **Zimmi/Zimme** — "You see me?" / "Do you understand me?" — used to check comprehension, similar to "you know?" punctuating discussion

## SECTION 7: PROVERBS & WISDOM

*Jamaican proverbs reflect African and European influences; they serve as road maps for life, teaching survival, patience, and wisdom through metaphor.*

### Essential Proverbs with Meanings

**"Every mickle mek a muckle"** / **"One one cocoa fill basket"**
- English: Every little bit adds up
- Use: Encouraging persistence, patience, step-by-step progress

**"Chicken merry, hawk deh near"**
- English: The chicken celebrates while the hawk is near
- Use: Warning — danger lurks even in happy times; stay vigilant

**"Duppy know who fi frighten"**
- English: The ghost knows who to scare
- Use: Bullies and opportunists pick on the weak; they won't test the strong

**"If yuh cyaan hear, yuh wi feel"**
- English: If you won't listen, you'll learn the hard way
- Use: Warning to the stubborn — consequences will teach what words couldn't

**"Time longer dan rope"**
- English: Time is longer than rope
- Use: Patience wins; there's enough time; don't rush

**"Wah sweet nanny goat a go run him belly"**
- English: What tastes good to the goat will ruin his belly
- Use: Things that seem good now can hurt you later; caution against temptation

**"Di higher di monkey climb, di more im expose"**
- English: The higher the monkey climbs, the more he's exposed
- Use: The more powerful or visible you become, the more scrutiny you face

**"Every hoe ha dem stick a bush"**
- English: To each his own; there is someone for everyone
- Use: Acceptance that different things/people suit different needs

**"Trouble nuh set like rain"**
- English: Trouble doesn't set up like rain (can't be predicted)
- Use: Problems can appear without warning; be prepared

**"Wanti wanti cyaan get it, getti getti nuh want it"**
- English: Those who desperately want something can't get it; those who always get things don't want them
- Use: Gratitude; don't take what you have for granted; count blessings

**"Tek sleep mark death"**
- English: Use sleep to understand death (pay attention to signs)
- Use: Don't be caught off guard; read the signs around you; stay alert

**"Nuh put all yuh egg inna one basket"**
- English: Don't put all your eggs in one basket
- Use: Diversify; don't risk everything on one bet

**"If yuh waan good, yuh nose haffi run"**
- English: If you want good things, your nose has to run (work hard)
- Use: Success requires sacrifice and hard work; no shortcuts

**"Dawg nuh howl if im have bone"**
- English: The dog doesn't howl if it has a bone
- Use: Satisfied people don't complain; give people what they need

**"Mi come yah fi drink milk, mi noh come yah fi count cow"**
- English: I came here to drink milk, not count cows
- Use: Conduct business in a straightforward manner; deliver what you promise

**"Cowad man kip soun bone"**
- English: A cowardly man keeps sound bones
- Use: Better to be thought of as a coward than to risk your life needlessly

**"New broom sweep clean, old broom know corna"**
- English: New brooms sweep clean, but old brooms know the corners
- Use: Experience matters; don't undervalue the wisdom of the experienced

**"Wa de goat du, de kid falla"**
- English: What the goat does, the kid follows
- Use: Children copy parents; set good examples

**"Yuh shake man han, yuh noh shake im hawt"**
- English: You can shake a man's hand but not his heart
- Use: Can't know what someone really thinks/feels by surface interaction

**"Yu cyaan sidung pahn cow bak cuss cow kin"**
- English: Can't sit on the cow's back and curse the cow's skin
- Use: Don't bite the hand that feeds you; ingratitude is wrong

**"Fire de a Mus Mus tail, im tink a cool breeze"**
- English: Set a rat's tail on fire; it thinks it's a cool breeze
- Use: Someone clueless about danger they're in; delusional complacency

**"Yuh neva see empty bag stan up"**
- English: You never see an empty bag stand up
- Use: Without resources, you cannot function; you need the basics to operate

**"A di quiet cow di butcher kill"**
- English: The quiet cow is the one the butcher kills
- Use: Silence can make you a target; being too passive has risks

**"Nuh cup nuh broke, nuh coffee nuh dash weh"**
- English: No cup broken, no coffee thrown away
- Use: When total disaster hasn't struck, count your blessings; don't exaggerate small issues

**"Tu much ratta nebba dig gud hole"**
- English: Too many rats never dug a good hole
- Use: Too many cooks spoil the broth; too many people on one task

**"Big blanket mek man sleep late"**
- English: A big blanket makes a man sleep late
- Use: Too many luxuries breed complacency and laziness

**"Yuh nuh wear boot fi ketch house fowl"**
- English: You don't wear boots to catch domestic chickens
- Use: Don't use disproportionate resources for simple tasks; overkill

**"Stranger no know where da deep water in de pass"**
- English: A stranger doesn't know where the deep water is in the road
- Use: Outsiders don't know local dangers; caution in unfamiliar territory

## SECTION 12: FOOD & JAMAICAN CULTURE REFERENCES

### National & Traditional Foods
- **Ackee and saltfish** — national dish of Jamaica; ackee = African fruit introduced 1778
- **Jerk** — spiced, slow-cooked method (jerk chicken, jerk pork)
- **Bammy** — cassava flatbread
- **Festival** — slightly sweet fried dough (not the event)
- **Rice and peas** — actually rice cooked with kidney beans or gungo peas (classic Sunday dish)
- **Callalou** — leafy green vegetable dish (like spinach stew)
- **Pepperpot** — rich stew
- **Bulla** — round sugar/flour cookie/cake
- **Dumplings** — boiled or fried dough; essential comfort food
- **Mannish water** — goat-head soup; said to have special properties
- **Escovitch fish** — fried fish marinated in vinegar with vegetables
- **Patty** — Jamaican beef (or other filling) pastry — the iconic fast food
- **Dasheen** — yam-like root vegetable
- **Coco** — edible root (taro/eddo family)
- **Breadfruit** — starchy tropical fruit; can be roasted, fried, or boiled
- **Plantain** — ripe or green; fried sweet plantain is beloved
- **Jelly** — young coconut with water and soft jelly inside
- **Pinda** — peanut
- **Asham** — parched, sweetened, ground corn
- **Dokunu/Tie-a-leaf** — dessert cooked in banana leaves

### Drinks
- **Sky juice** — water from a bag; also: flavored ice in a bag
- **Roots** — herbal tonic drink; various roots and herbs brewed together
- **Rum** — ubiquitous; Jamaica produces some of the world's finest rum (Appleton, J. Wray)

### Places
- **Kingston** — capital city; center of music, politics, culture
- **Trenchtown** — Bob Marley's neighborhood; birthplace of ska and reggae
- **Portmore** — Vybz Kartel's hometown; "Gaza" epicenter
- **Spanish Town** — oldest city; former colonial capital
- **Montego Bay (Mobay)** — tourist hub, second city
- **Negril** — resort town, beautiful beaches
- **Nine Miles** — Bob Marley's birthplace and burial site in St. Ann
- **Cockpit Country** — Maroon stronghold; dense interior
